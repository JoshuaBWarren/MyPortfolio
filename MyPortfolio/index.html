Pathfinder

Project Exodus

Pathfinder is a Power Platform based application designed to enable a single source of truth for Portfolios, Programs, Projects, and Change Management. Certain benefits include:

  • Rapid prototyping and development
  • Personalization capabilities
  • One intake point for multiple types of work
  • Portfolio view of top-level initiatives
  • Allows teams to manage their work in their tools (like Azure Dev Ops or ADO)
The gist of the project was that the organization had projects and work being tracked and ran from various sources and silo's. There wasn't a, "single source of truth", where one could go to get an overview of what the status was of any given project being worked on. Some teams were using ADO, or Microsoft Project, or even tracking it via Microsoft Power Point and Excel. Getting a status involved messaging the team involved and going down the ladder until you were able to get an answer.

These projects are tracked and statuses gathered for use during an Monthly Business Review (MBR). Without a single source, Business Managers were forced to spend significant amounts of time tracking the statuses and getting it ready for the MBR.
Project Exodus would solve this problem by migrating all of the data for all the work the organization does to a single source: Pathfinder. To be successful, I would need to perform the following:

  1. Identify all of the projects, where they live, and find all the data related to the project.
  2. Keep track of the list of projects and update any new ones that get created during the project.
  3. Gather org requirements and manage their implementation into the Pathfinder app.
  4. Coordinate testing and validation of implemented requirements.
  5. Communicate and train stakeholders in the use of Pathfinder.
  6. Perform the physical migration and move all of the data.
  7. Verify and test to ensure all of the data was uploaded successfuly.
  8. Control and monitor the app for bugs and identify any ways it can be improved.

Project Exodus required knowing what types of work were we going to track in Pathfinder. To do this, I identified stakeholders and gathered and met with leadership to figure this out and the result were fairly simple:

  • If it's a Microsoft led effort by FTE's whether a project or run-state, it's in Pathfinder
  • If it's a v-team (vendor team) initiative being run by an FTE or otherwise not Microsot led, it's not in Pathfinder
Next, I met with reprentatives of the teams and those running projects to identify which of their projects conform to the new standards and which ones can stay wherever it was being managed. The deliverable from the step was the Exodus Project List. It was a living document that everybody had access to. Stakeholders would update the list with projects completed or any new projects that were created or started during Project Exodus.

During this team, I had met with the org leadership to identify what were the requirements needed from the app to house their project data. My task here was to take this list to the developers of Pathfinder, track their implementation, and test it to ensure it complies. This process was slow as the org has very specific requirements that was tricky to implement. During this time I updated leadership on a weekly basis to ensure visibility into the progress.

Once implementation was complete, I created and maintained a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) to aid in training. At the same time, I initiated the migration of the projects from their sources and into Pathfinder. Once I verified that the migration was a success, I held Pathfinder training as well as open office hours for continuous support.

CK Employee Website

A more efficient and simple solution.

My role when first hired by this company was to be the Teacher Supervisor. I was to interview, manage, mentor, and train teachers who go to schools in the Greater Seattle area to teach kids how to code.

I immediately noticed that the employee website that teachers used to log hours and get information about the school they were teaching at as well as the curriculum they were teaching, was big and bulky. After interviewing many of my teachers, I came to discover that many teachers didn't even use the website for anything other than the time sheets. I was also privy to many conversations with administrators about how frustrated they were about the site.

After conducting many interviews with users and other stakeholders, I crafted an organized and well thought out plan complete with a design (above) to pitch to the owner of the company. My project was authorized soon after.

From my research, ease of access was the most sought after feature for the site. Both administrators and general users wanted to get to where they needed to go in as few clicks as possible while also being able to get more information within those clicks. First thing that I did was I created a UX test that had three tasks. A user would take this test to complete the three tasks as I timed it. This would provide me a starting metric to test to verify project success.

I created all of the Epics, User Stories, and tasks using Azure DevOps (ADO). For teachers, I changed their landing page to be a calendar. The calendar would then populate with that specific teachers classes for the month. Teachers could click onto that specific class to get more information. Whichever class they clicked into would present a new page that has the schools contact information, address, Point of Contact, as well as a space for teachers to be able to add new information such as, "Don't park in this parking lot after 5" or "New construction on Main street, take 2nd street to get inside.".

For administrators, they were to use the same landing page they had been using before, but with a rearranging of informatin. All links that had nothing to do with that specific administartors rights would not be visible and all unnecessary files were archived. The time sheet page also added more functionality to users by giving them the ability to edit past entires (up to a week) and to be able to add in miles driven.

During the project, I communicated heavily with users and stakeholders to keep them up-to-date on the progress as well as to attain feedback. After the project, the same UX test from before was conducted and each user that took the test showed an huge decrease in time required to complete tasks.

Onsite Calibration Technican Training Program

A "how-to" BC guide for new hires!

Due to a signed agreement, I am unable to provide any pictures or screenshots of this proejct.

When I was first hired on by BC Group International, I was taken into the Calibration Lab where I met all of the calibration technicians by the Service Manager. After the pleasantries, the Service Manager left and that was it. That was when I discovered that BC Group didn't have a formal training program for incomming new hires nor was there any sort of process in play that would dictate what happens when new hires arrive. Without any direction, I began working on a project to remedy this.

I took it upon myself to begin grabbing equipment and asking the Calibartion Technicians how to work on them. This was the beginning of my training and I took comprehensive notes. I focused on the two main aspects of the training program that a new hire would need before traveling to hospital sites: the tech side and the admin side.

The tech side had to do with all of the biomedical equipment a new hire might encounter while working. Anything from multimeters and safety analyers to defibrillator's and ESU analyers, I began to compile technical documentation to create standardized SOP's for new hires to use when they encounter the equipment. The SOP's also came with tech notes that dove into the specifications of the equipment, which resistors to turn, and when a piece of equipment was no longer qualified to be calibrated and needed repairs or replacement.

The admin side had to do with a program called, "Alpha". This program was used by the technician to log information, create calibartion certificates, as well as keep an up-to-date list on the equipment a site had. Alpha was a DOS program and required training in order to use.

I combined all of this into a training book complete with screenshotes of Alpha, SOP's, as well as a recommended training timeline for new hires to use. The goal of the project was to make it so that all the Service Manger had to do was hand them the training book and the New Hire could begin their training immediately.

As a project metric, I used myself. It took me six months of teaching myself as well as getting outside aid before I could take a van and begin working as an Onsite Calibration Technician. A trainee was hired on and used my training guide and was able to complete everything and get on the road within three months, a 50% decrease.

Global Broadcast Service (GBS) Antenna Replacement

Installation and testing of a new GRC-211 antenna

As the senior technician for my division onboard the USS Enterprise as well as one of the few techs onboard who was knowledgable and qualified to work on the GBS system, I played a critical role in the replacement, installation, and testing of a new GRC-211 antenna.

During deployment, the GRC-211 antenna had been damanged within the dome. The damage was unable to be repaired out at sea and on closer inspection it was discovered that the damage itself was such that the entire antenna array needed to be replaced.

I coordinated with military contractors as well as shore side work crews to coordinate the process. After getting the proper permissions, contractors were able to remove the old antenna array so that it could be lifted off the ship via crane. The company that we used to purchase the new GRC-211 array arrived and we used the crane to lift it up to the top of the mast while contractors, as well as my junior sailors, installed it.

After installation, I lead the team and worked with contractors to test the system and ensure everything was installed correctly and that the entire system was functioning as it should.
